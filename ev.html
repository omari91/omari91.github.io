Chapter I: The Foundational Model (Paper 1)

This chapter is based on the initial work: **Enabling Equitable EV Charger Deployment in Berlin with Multi-Objective Geospatial Optimization**. It established the core challenge: balancing maximum charging coverage (Utility) with fair distribution (Equity).

1. Defining Utility and Equity in Berlin

Paper 1 modeled Berlin's EV charging problem using crisp (deterministic) values for demand and budget. The key contribution was defining the two critical, conflicting policy objectives for the city's 12 districts.

The Objectives of the Crisp Model (Paper 1)

Objective 1: Maximize Utility
City-wide Coverage
(Maximize total EV coverage for the city)
↔
Objective 2: Maximize Equity
Fair Distribution
(Minimize Gini Index gap between districts)

This trade-off yielded the **Crisp Pareto Front**, but its plans were only optimal if demand predictions were 100% accurate.

2. The Crucial Flaw: The Fragile Solution

The deterministic model in Paper 1 failed to account for two critical real-world factors: the uncertainty in future EV adoption rates and the flexibility in planning budgets. This created **brittle plans** that could suffer massive performance drops if assumptions proved wrong.

Why the Deterministic Plan is Brittle (The Motivation for Paper 2)

1. Uncertain Demand

EV adoption is a wide range, not a single number.

→
2. Flexible Budget

Budgets often have an acceptable range of investment.

→
3. Outcome Risk

A plan optimized for the 'best guess' fails spectacularly in 'worst-case' scenarios.

Addressing Brittleness: The Move to Robustness (Paper 2)

The weakness of the initial plan necessitated a new, higher-level framework. Paper 2 introduces the **Fuzzy Multi-Objective Optimization** approach—explicitly modeling the uncertainty of demand and budget—to guarantee a **resilient and reliable** EV network, regardless of future volatility.

Chapter II: The Robust Solution (Paper 2)

This chapter details the methodology and superior results of applying the **Fuzzy Robust Optimization Framework**. The focus shifts from simply optimizing for a *single best case* to optimizing for the *highest guaranteed minimum performance*.

3. The Fuzzy Robust Optimization Framework

Paper 2 solved the brittleness problem by replacing single values with **Triangular Fuzzy Numbers (TFNs)** for uncertain parameters. This allowed the optimization algorithm (E-NSGAII) to find a set of solutions—the **Fuzzy Pareto Front**—that are robust across the full range of possible futures.

Steps of the Robust-Fuzzy Solution Procedure

1. Model Fuzzification

Demand & budget are modeled as ranges (TFNs)

→
2. Robust Defuzzification

Use Chance-Constrained Programming for objective satisfaction

→
3. Multi-Objective Solver

Apply the E-NSGAII Algorithm to find the Robust Pareto Front

6. The Path Forward: From Policy to Pavement

While the framework delivers a powerful policy-level strategy, continued research is vital to transition to a fully deployable engineering solution. Future work involves integrating deeper technical and behavioral data.

Grid Integration

Couple the model with power flow simulations to ensure technical feasibility and avoid localized grid congestion.

Finer-Grained Equity

Incorporate socio-economic data (income, housing type) to address crucial inequalities within existing districts.

Behavioral Modeling

Integrate dynamic user behaviors like adaptive routing and price sensitivity for more realistic demand forecasting.
